Officials in Japan have issued a stark warning regarding an increased risk of a "huge" earthquake in the coming week. This follows a powerful 7.7 magnitude earthquake that struck off the north-east coast on Monday, triggering massive evacuation orders and a series of tsunami warnings.

The initial quake occurred in the waters off Iwate Prefecture, approximately 530km (330 miles) north of Tokyo. Residents along the northern coast were advised to seek higher ground immediately as tsunami warnings predicted waves of up to 3 metres (10 feet). While the largest waves recorded reached 80cm and many warnings have since been lifted, the japan earthquake today tsunami warning remains a critical concern for the region.

Mass Evacuations and Travel Disruption

More than 170,000 people across several prefectures, including Hokkaido, Aomori, and Fukushima, were ordered to evacuate. The earthquake today caused significant disruption to Japan's infrastructure. The Tohoku Shinkansen bullet train services were suspended between Tokyo and Shin-Aomori, and local rail lines in Iwate faced complete shutdowns.

According to reports from the Japan Meteorological Agency (JMA), quakes "causing even stronger shaking" could occur within the next week. Authorities have stated that the risk of a quake measuring 8.0 magnitude or higher is "relatively higher than during normal times," keeping the nation on high alert for japan earthquakes.

Is it Safe to Travel to Japan?

For those questioning if it is safe to travel to Japan right now, authorities urge extreme caution. While bullet train services have partially resumed, tremors were felt as far away as Tokyo, and the situation remains volatile. The japan tsunami advisories emphasize that waves can hit repeatedly, and residents are urged not to leave safe ground until all warnings are officially cleared.

The memories of the devastating 2011 earthquake and subsequent Fukushima nuclear disaster remain fresh in the minds of the Japanese people. Fortunately, no abnormalities have been reported at key coastal nuclear plants following the latest japan earthquake, but structural assessments are still ongoing.

Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi has urged the public to stay vigilant and move to "higher, safer places" at the first sign of trouble.
